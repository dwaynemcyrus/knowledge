## What to Teach
> As one who has gone through the work of healing as a childhood and adult sexual abuse survivor, what can I teach others who are behind me in their journey?

As someone who has gone through the journey of healing from childhood and adult sexual abuse, you have a wealth of experience and insights that can be incredibly valuable to others who are at earlier stages in their journey. Here are some key areas you can focus on teaching others:

### 1. **Understanding Trauma and Its Impact**
   - **Teach:** The nature of trauma, how it affects the brain and body, and the common emotional and psychological symptoms that survivors experience.
   - **Why It's Important:** Understanding the science behind trauma can help survivors feel less alone and more empowered to seek help.

### 2. **Creating a Safe Space**
   - **Teach:** How to establish a safe environment, both physically and emotionally, where they can begin their healing process.
   - **Why It's Important:** Safety is a fundamental need for healing. Knowing how to create and maintain a safe space is crucial for trauma survivors.

### 3. **Self-Compassion and Self-Care**
   - **Teach:** Techniques for self-compassion, self-acceptance, and self-care practices that support healing.
   - **Why It's Important:** Survivors often struggle with self-blame and guilt. Learning to be kind to themselves is a significant step towards healing.

### 4. **Recognizing and Setting Boundaries**
   - **Teach:** The importance of boundaries in relationships and how to set and maintain them.
   - **Why It's Important:** Boundaries protect survivors from further harm and help them establish healthier relationships.

### 5. **Finding and Using Support Systems**
   - **Teach:** How to identify supportive people and communities, and the importance of professional help such as therapy or counseling.
   - **Why It's Important:** Support systems provide the necessary emotional backing and practical advice needed to navigate the healing journey.

### 6. **Coping Strategies and Resilience Building**
   - **Teach:** Effective coping mechanisms for dealing with triggers, flashbacks, and overwhelming emotions.
   - **Why It's Important:** Coping strategies help survivors manage their symptoms and build resilience.

### 7. **Reclaiming Control and Empowerment**
   - **Teach:** Techniques to reclaim control over their lives, such as decision-making skills, assertiveness training, and empowerment exercises.
   - **Why It's Important:** Regaining a sense of control is vital for rebuilding self-esteem and confidence.

### 8. **Healing Through Creative Expression**
   - **Teach:** How to use creative outlets like writing, art, music, or dance to express emotions and process trauma.
   - **Why It's Important:** Creative expression can be a powerful tool for healing and self-discovery.

### 9. **Mindfulness and Meditation**
   - **Teach:** Mindfulness practices and meditation techniques that can help survivors stay grounded and present.
   - **Why It's Important:** Mindfulness can reduce anxiety, improve emotional regulation, and enhance overall well-being.

### 10. **Setting and Achieving Personal Goals**
   - **Teach:** How to set realistic, achievable goals for their personal and professional lives, and create a plan to reach them.
   - **Why It's Important:** Setting and achieving goals gives survivors a sense of purpose and direction.

## Norms to Change
[[Enemy to Fight|New ways of being]] to get Samira to see and desire the value in for her life.

For sexual abuse survivors seeking transformation and improved wellbeing, certain accepted norms or beliefs may need to change. Here’s a list of norms that could be addressed to facilitate healing and personal growth:

1. Self-Blame and Guilt
	- Old Norm: Believing the abuse was their fault or that they did something to deserve it.
	- New Norm: Understanding that the abuse was not their fault and shifting the blame to the abuser.
1. Silence and Secrecy
	- Old Norm: Keeping the abuse a secret out of fear, shame, or a sense of duty to protect others.
	- New Norm: Recognizing the importance of speaking out and seeking support to break the cycle of silence.
2. Low Self-Worth
	- Old Norm: Accepting feelings of worthlessness or inadequacy as normal.
	- New Norm: Cultivating self-worth and acknowledging their inherent value and strengths.
3. Distrust in Relationships
	- Old Norm: Assuming that all relationships will be harmful or that trust is impossible.
	- New Norm: Learning to build trust and recognize healthy relationships and boundaries.
4. Avoidance of Emotions
	- Old Norm: Suppressing or avoiding emotions to cope with pain.
	- New Norm: Embracing and processing emotions as a path to healing and understanding oneself.
5. Isolation
	- Old Norm: Isolating oneself to avoid vulnerability or protect against further harm.
	- New Norm: Engaging in supportive communities and relationships to foster connection and healing.
6. Fear of Vulnerability
	- Old Norm: Believing that being vulnerable is dangerous and must be avoided.
	- New Norm: Understanding that vulnerability is a strength and a key to forming meaningful connections.
7. Negative Self-Talk
	- Old Norm: Engaging in negative self-talk and internal criticism.
	- New Norm: Practicing positive self-talk and self-compassion.
8. Perfectionism
	- Old Norm: Believing that they must be perfect to be accepted or loved.
	- New Norm: Embracing imperfections and focusing on progress and self-acceptance.
10.	Avoiding Help
	- Old Norm: Feeling that seeking help is a sign of weakness.
	- New Norm: Recognizing that seeking help is a courageous and necessary step toward healing.
11.	Over-Functioning
	- Old Norm: Overworking or overachieving to compensate for feelings of inadequacy.
	- New Norm: Striving for balance and self-care, acknowledging that their worth is not tied to productivity.
12.	Detachment from the Body
	- Old Norm: Disconnecting from their body due to trauma-related dissociation or discomfort.
	- New Norm: Reconnecting with their body through mindfulness, physical activity, and self-care practices.
13.	Fear of Intimacy
	- Old Norm: Avoiding intimacy due to fear of being hurt again.
	- New Norm: Gradually allowing themselves to experience intimacy in safe, healthy relationships.
14.	Minimizing Trauma
	- Old Norm: Downplaying the impact of the abuse to cope or avoid confrontation.
	- New Norm: Validating their experiences and acknowledging the impact of trauma as a step toward healing.

By addressing and transforming these norms, survivors can begin to heal and build a more fulfilling, empowered life.
